## What is screening in?

Screening in is the process of evaluating potential candidates to identify the best match for the job. It is an inclusive approach to recruitment that focuses on candidates’ skills and competencies—even if they don’t fit all the criteria required for a role.

Such a focus allows the candidates to showcase their skills and provide additional context that often gets lost in a traditional resume or cover letter.

## Benefits of screening in 

On average, recruiters take 42 days to fill a role. Hence, finding the right candidates who check all the boxes is difficult.

Benefits of screening in

 
Transitioning from rigid traditional practices to a more flexible screening-in approach can significantly benefit companies.

Screening-in focuses on candidates who may not have the perfect credentials or a traditional background but have the **skills and potential** to excel in the role. 
 
This approach helps companies tap into capable talent they might otherwise overlook, offering a more **inclusive and effective** way to find the right fit. 

Such an approach inevitably promotes inclusivity and diversity. Since 32% of candidates prefer companies that emphasize diversity in their workplace, this is a win-win situation for both employers and candidates. 

## Disadvantages of screening in 

A major con of the screening in method is that it is **time-consuming**. Recruiters may find themselves with an extensive list of potential candidates that need to be screened individually, requiring a thorough review of each resume and CV. 

Disadvantages of screening in

While this approach can be effective, it is only practical when a dedicated hiring team has ample resources and time to manage the screening process efficiently.

## What is screening out?

Screening out refers to eliminating candidates based on specific qualifications or criteria. This process helps narrow the applicant pool to find the most suitable candidate for hiring. 
 
The main objective of screening out is to quickly categorize the talent pool and identify and remove candidates who may not fit the organization well. 
 
Since this approach focuses on eliminating candidates, screening out can result in overlooking great talent who may possess the skills and potential to succeed in the role. 

It is often seen as a method focused on finding reasons *not* to hire someone.

## Benefits of screening out

The screening out method is beneficial when searching for candidates with technical proficiency. It helps **eliminate unqualified candidates** in the first step.

Benefits of screening out

For instance, candidates must possess essential, non-negotiable skills in technical roles such as Java or Python software developers. 

Applications that fail to meet these specific criteria can be automatically filtered out using an ATS or other screening technology.

However, there’s an essential distinction between screening out a candidate who lacks a required license and one who simply doesn’t hold a particular degree or level of education. 

The former is often non-negotiable, while the latter might exclude qualified candidates who possess the skills but don’t fit traditional academic requirements.

Additionally, if a candidate **doesn’t meet the basic requirements** for a role, you can screen out them. 

For example, if the position is up, especially for a time zone, location, etc. However, understand that screening out should not be used as a discriminatory way to eliminate any candidates. 

Screening-out techniques help to filter resumes that may have slipped past the spam filter, especially when dealing with a large influx of applications.

This is common in MNCs, where a single job posting often attracts many candidates.

## Disadvantages of screening out

There are a few downsides to the screening out method.

Disadvantages of screening out

Firstly, since screening out focuses on eliminating candidates quickly, it often results in **unconscious biases**.  
 
Recruiters might start evaluating candidates based on gender, ethnicity, demographics, and other irrelevant personal factors. Such bias-based hiring leads to bad hires, eventually hampering productivity, employee engagement, and the company’s success. 
 
A single bad hire can cost your company approximately $15,000, and about 74% of hiring professionals agree to hire the wrong candidate. This signifies the importance of implementing more inclusive and objective hiring practices. 
 
Another potential con of screening out is that recruiters sometimes **use the wrong metrics** to assess a candidate. 

This often arises from a misalignment between the hiring manager’s expectations and the recruiters’ understanding of those requirements.

## What is the difference between screening in vs screening out?

Screening in and screening out are both screening methods that differ in their purpose. Screening in is used to find the potential candidate while screening out is used to eliminate candidates who don’t meet the criteria. 
 
The screening-in approach is precious in a competitive job market, where employers should consider candidates who may not tick every box but still bring essential skills and qualities. 

On the other hand, screening out aims to simplify the hiring process by ensuring that only qualified candidates move on to the subsequent evaluation stages.

## Examples of screening in vs screening out 

The best example of screening in is when recruiters look at a candidate’s profile and ask, “What qualities does this person possess that could fill our current gaps or add value?” 
 
A screen-out mindset example involves evaluating a candidate’s profile by asking, “What aspects of this person do not meet my criteria?” 
 
The best outreach a recruiter can take between screening in vs screening out should be “screening-in”. This is because instead of trying to eliminate candidates who don’t meet the entire criteria, screen in tries to find reasons why they would qualify. 

## Where should screening in and screening out be used?

Companies use screening in method to retain the top candidates.

Where should be screening in used

Scenarios where **screen-in method is used** include:

1. **Limited applications**: When a job posting receives fewer applicants and recruiters have to settle for the *next best* candidate, a screen-in strategy can be used. 
2. **Final evaluation stage**: This stage involves adding insights about what is already known about the candidate.
3. **Integrated Approach**: When hiring candidates requires integrated data, the screen-in method can be used. This utilizes multiple data sources to inform decisions rather than relying on a single factor (e.g., behavioural assessments, psychometric assessments, etc.).

Below are some of the situations where screening out can be used.

Where should be screening out used

1. **High-volume selection**: When faced with large candidate pools that overwhelm traditional recruiting methods, implementing successive pass/fail milestones can streamline the process, reducing the number of candidates considered at each stage.
2. **Lower-stakes positions**: For roles that demand fewer resources and where performance is easy to measure, decisions can be automated through assessments, screening out unqualified candidates.
3. **High-stakes testing**: This approach ensures that only qualified individuals are selected for positions with significant consequences for failure.

When a candidate is described as “screened in” by HR, it means they have successfully passed the initial evaluation stage of the recruitment process. This indicates that the candidate meets the qualifications and criteria for the position and is considered a potential fit for further consideration, such as interviews.
