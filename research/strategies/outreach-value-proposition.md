## 1. What it is
The single most important rule for outreach emails: lead with what the *other person* gets, not what
*you* want. WIIFM stands for "what's in it for me" — and you have to answer that question *from their
side* before you ask for anything.

In plain terms: don't open with "please give me a link." Open with "here's something valuable for
you," then make your request.

## 2. Why it works
- Everyone judges incoming requests by "what's in it for me?" Gotch says ~99% of the 10–20 outreach
  emails he gets daily are all about the sender — so a genuine, value-first offer instantly stands out.
- The offer itself is the "lead domino" — the one thing that knocks everything else over. Clever
  tactics (subject lines, timing) only help *after* the offer is good.

## 3. When to use / prerequisites
- Any outreach-based acquisition: applies on top of [[strategies/link-prospecting-prioritization]],

## 4. End-to-end process
1. For each prospect, define the **offer/value** before writing — what do *they* gain?
2. Choose from the five proven value propositions below (pick the one that fits the prospect):
3. Frame the pitch as a win-win: "I'll do X for you; would you be willing to do Y?"
4. Layer on technical hygiene (secondary): email warm-up, send velocity, positioning, copywriting.

### The five proven value propositions (source 0002)

**1. Extremely high-quality original content**
Let people know about genuinely unique, share-worthy content you've created. They may not link
immediately — but they might share it on social media or in their newsletter. That exposure gets
your asset in front of more people, which can eventually lead to backlinks.
*Best for:* cold outreach to publishers and bloggers; works even when the prospect has no direct
incentive to link right now.

**2. Money**
Offer direct compensation in exchange for a backlink / sponsored placement.
*Best for:* prospects who are open to paid placements. Note: paying for links can conflict with
search-engine guidelines — flag every paid link as "Sponsored" where required and vet the site
quality carefully. Gotch calls this "hands down the number one value proposition" in practice.

**3. Exposure**
Offer to promote *their* work to your audience (social share, email mention, newsletter feature)
or bring them on for an interview (see [[strategies/get-interviewed]] — the "Oprah Technique").
*Prerequisite:* only works if you have an actual audience the other person values. A brand-new
site with no following cannot credibly offer exposure. Works very well once you have a list,
podcast audience, or social following.

**4. Free Help**
Offer something genuinely useful at no cost — pointing out a broken link on their site, alerting
them to a hacked or defaced page, fixing a technical issue, or notifying them of outdated
information. The help is the value; the link request is secondary.
*Best for:* [[strategies/content-resurrection]] (broken link outreach) and cold outreach where
you've spotted a real problem on their site. Low friction ask because you're giving before taking.

**5. Free Content**
Write content they need but haven't published yet (guest posting), add value to an existing
article (suggest an update or a new section), or help refresh old content. They get a quality
piece without spending their own time; you get an editorial link.
*Best for:* guest post pitches, niche-edit / link-insert requests, [[strategies/content-replacement]]
outreach.

## 6. Success metrics
- Reply rate, positive-reply rate, link conversion rate.
- (Diagnose poor results at the value-proposition level first, not micro-tactics.)

## 7. Risks & pitfalls
- Self-centered pitches ("I need a link").
- Over-tuning micro-tactics while the offer itself is weak.
- Paid incentives must still respect quality and guideline considerations — see [[strategies/linkable-asset-promotion]].
